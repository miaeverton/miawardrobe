# STYLING PRINCIPLES LEARNED FROM MY STYLIST
## Extracted from 61 lookbook boards — Spring + Winter 2026

*These are observed, repeated patterns — not generic fashion advice. They override generic styling logic in every recommendation.*

---

## FOOTWEAR PRINCIPLES

**F1 — The shoe controls the occasion, not the outfit.**
The same outfit was shown with 2–5 different shoes on a single board, each shifting the occasion level. Shoe hierarchy casual → evening: high-top sneaker / flat sandal / ballet flat / mary jane / slingback flat / kitten heel slingback / kitten heel boot.

**F2 — The wrong-shoe theory is applied intentionally.**
Sneakers with formal or feminine pieces are deliberate styling choices. A Chuck Taylor with a satin skirt reads intentional only when the rest of the outfit is polished.

**F3 — Flat shoes are not a downgrade.**
~85% of boards use flat or very low-heeled footwear. Flats are the dominant aesthetic choice, not a compromise.

**F4 — Snakeskin is a neutral.**
Snakeskin slingbacks and slides function as quiet luxury accents, not statement shoes.

**F5 — The kitten heel is the primary occasion-elevator.**
When shifting an outfit from daytime to evening without changing garments, the move is always a kitten heel slingback.

## PROPORTION PRINCIPLES

**P1 — Volume lives in one place only.**
Every outfit has one dominant shape. Voluminous bottom = minimal top. Voluminous top = straight bottom. Never volume on both ends simultaneously.

**P2 — Wide-leg silhouette is non-negotiable.**
Every trouser, jean, and pant is wide-leg or barrel-cut. No exceptions.

**P3 — The cropped top with a full bottom is a core formula.**
Strapless tube top + wide-leg jeans. Fitted tank + full skirt. Creates natural waist definition.

**P4 — Long lines are protected.**
Nothing interrupts a long vertical line — no busy accessory, no belt where it doesn't belong, no bag worn in a way that cuts the silhouette.

## LAYERING PRINCIPLES

**L1 — Open shirts are always layers, never standalone tops.**
Oversized shirts (black linen, dark denim) are worn open over a tucked-in tank or tee. The inner is always visible and intentional.

**L2 — The visible inner layer must be clean and minimal.**
Under an open shirt: ribbed tank, fitted tee, or strapless top only. Never a patterned or loose inner.

**L3 — The blazer anchors, it does not finish.**
The ivory blazer is used to shift formality upward. The outfit underneath must work without the blazer.

**L4 — Layering reduces complexity, it does not add it.**
Every layered look has fewer competing elements than it appears. Layering is editing, not accumulation.

**L5 — Blazer is the only layer over a dress.**
No cardigans, shirts, or scarves over dresses. Blazer only.

## COLOR PRINCIPLES

**C1 — One color accent at a time.**
When a color piece appears (red sandal, green bag, cobalt cardigan, orange dress), everything else is completely neutral.

**C2 — Color dresses take neutral accessories only.**
Kelly green dress, orange gathered dress: black bag, gold jewellery, white or natural sandal. No color-on-color.

**C3 — The black/white contrast signature.**
When the outfit skews light, the bag or anchor piece goes black. When the outfit is dark, one white or cream element (shoe, sandal, belt) creates the necessary lightness.

**C4 — Tonal dressing uses fabric contrast, not flat matching.**
Cream knit + chocolate silk skirt. Grey cardigan + black silk trousers. The colors are related; the fabrics are always different.

---

## BAG PRINCIPLES

**B1 — The bag must not compete with the outfit.**
Bag is chosen to support the palette. It introduces the same family, not a new element.

**B2 — The black bucket bag is the default neutral.**
Works with everything, reads smart without being precious.

**B3 — Coastal/woven bags signal casual register.**
Raffia clutches, woven drawstring bags, wicker totes — casual and vacation only.

**B4 — Evening bags are small and structured.**
Black patent clutch or small structured pouch for evening. Size decreases as formality increases.

---

## JEWELLERY PRINCIPLES

**J1 — Jewellery finishes, it does not feature.**
Gold pieces are architectural finishers, not competing statements.

**J2 — Evening combination: gold disc earring + gold bangle.**
The Alexis Bittar pairing. Recurring across all evening boards.

**J3 — Daytime combination: pearl drop earring.**
Mejuri pearl drop hoops for smart casual and brunch.

**J4 — Maximum two jewellery pieces simultaneously.**
Earrings + one bracelet, or earrings + necklace. Never three.

**J5 — Sunglasses signal casual/outdoor register only.**
Never on blazer, work, or evening boards.

---

## BELT PRINCIPLES

**BT1 — The belt is a personality switch.**
Black belt = minimalist. Cream belt = tonal continuity. Leopard belt = personality. Brown belt = warm neutral. Tan belt = California ease.

**BT2 — Belt when the waist needs defining, not as decoration.**
Only when a relaxed top + high-waist bottom creates an undefined waist.

**BT3 — Never belt a blazer.**
Zero lookbook boards show a belt over the blazer.

**BT4 — Offer two belt options when a belt is appropriate.**
One minimal (black or cream), one with personality (leopard or brown).

## OUTERWEAR PRINCIPLES

**O1 — Outerwear is optional, never load-bearing.**
The outfit underneath always works without the coat or blazer.

**O2 — The cream duster coat IS the outfit.**
When the duster is worn, everything beneath is quiet. All-black or warm-neutral base, minimal accessories.

**O3 — Chore coats are the casual version of the blazer.**
Same anchoring principle, weekend register.

---

## SKIRT PRINCIPLES

**SK1 — The black silk maxi skirt is the most versatile bottom.**
Pairs with almost any top. The blank canvas of the wardrobe.

**SK2 — The white midi skirt always needs a dark anchor.**
Black top, black bag, or black shoes — always.

**SK3 — Full/pleated skirts require minimal tops.**
The one-volume rule at maximum strictness.

**SK4 — Sneakers with skirts is a deliberate signature move.**
Chuck Taylor or clean sneaker with a maxi/midi skirt = intentional. Must be the only casual element.

---

## DRESS PRINCIPLES

**DR1 — The black T-shirt maxi dress is the most versatile piece.**
Shoe choice controls the occasion entirely. Always offer multiple shoe options.

**DR2 — Color dresses take neutral accessories only.**

**DR3 — The ivory blazer over a black dress = day-to-evening formula.**

**DR4 — Dresses are complete outfits.**
Do not over-accessorize. The dress + one shoe + one bag is often enough.

**DR5 — Never belt a dress.**
No lookbook board belts a dress.

## CORE OUTFIT FORMULAS (the six validated bases)

| Formula | Pieces | Register |
|---|---|---|
| A — The Uniform | White/cream tee + wide-leg jeans + black bucket bag | Casual |
| B — The Blazer Edit | Ivory blazer + black tank + wide-leg jeans | Smart casual |
| C — The Black Column | Black silk maxi skirt + any black top | Smart casual → evening |
| D — The Tonal Brown | Cream/camel knit + brown trousers + brown accessories | Smart casual |
| E — The Layered Black | Black shirt open + white tank tucked + wide-leg jeans | Casual → smart casual |
| F — The Statement Dress | Colour/maxi dress + neutral accessories only | Casual → evening |

## THREE AB MOVES NOT IN THE LOOKBOOKS — EXECUTE THESE

1. **Open cardigan as a soft layer** over tank + jeans (softer version of the open-shirt formula)
2. **Oxford/penny loafer with a maxi dress** (the AB wrong-shoe move for dresses)
3. **Half tuck** on button-downs and oversized shirts over jeans

These were identified as gaps in the lookbook vocabulary. They have been executed in evolution_outfits.html and should be used going forward.

## WHAT THE STYLIST SYSTEMATICALLY AVOIDED

- Two competing silhouettes in one outfit
- Adding color to a color outfit
- Over-accessorizing (max two jewellery pieces)
- Bag that competes with the outfit
- Load-bearing outerwear
- Belt over a blazer
- Flat shoes as compromise (they are always presented as choices)
- Generic "add a statement necklace"
- "Pop of colour" bag on an unresolved outfit
- Long cardigan as an outer layer
- Ankle boots with skirts
- Literal matching of shoe to bag
- Belting a dress
- Scarves
